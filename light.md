Light exhibits both particle-like and wave-like properties, a concept known as wave-particle duality. When we refer to its wave structure, several characteristics 
come into play:

1. **Wave Equation**: Light is governed by Maxwell's equations, which describe how electric and magnetic fields propagate through space as electromagnetic waves. The 
general form of the electromagnetic wave equation in free space (in a vacuum) can be written as:
   \[
   \nabla^2 \mathbf{E} - \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial^2 \mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2} = 0
   \]
   where \( \mathbf{E} \) is the electric field, \( \mu_0 \) is the permeability of free space, and \( \varepsilon_0 \) is the permittivity of free space.

2. **Wavelength (\(\lambda\))**: The wavelength is the distance between successive crests (or troughs) of a wave. It can be calculated using the relationship:
   \[
   v = f \cdot \lambda
   \]
   where \( v \) is the speed of light, and \( f \) is the frequency.

3. **Frequency (\(f\))**: Frequency refers to how many cycles or oscillations occur in a unit of time. The human eye can detect frequencies roughly between 400 THz 
(corresponding to red light) and 700 THz (corresponding to violet light).

4. **Amplitude**: This is the height of the wave, which determines its intensity or brightness.

5. **Polarization**: Light waves are electromagnetic in nature, meaning they oscillate perpendicular to the direction of propagation. Polarization refers to the 
orientation of these electric field vectors. For example, linear polarization means that the electric field vector vibrates in a single plane, while circular 
polarization involves elliptical paths.

6. **Phase**: The phase describes the position within one cycle of a wave and can vary along its path. When two waves are in phase (their peaks and troughs align), 
they interfere constructively, leading to increased brightness or intensity. Conversely, when out of phase (peaks meet troughs), destructive interference occurs.

7. **Wavefront**: The wavefront is the set of points that oscillate in unison at a given moment. In an ideal vacuum, light travels along straight lines forming planar 
wavefronts. Near obstacles or boundaries, the wavefront can bend due to refraction.

8. **Interference and Diffraction**: These are phenomena where waves interact with each other or with apertures. Interference occurs when two waves overlap and 
combine their amplitudes, leading to areas of constructive interference (bright spots) and destructive interference (dark spots). Diffraction refers to the bending of 
waves around obstacles or through openings larger than a fraction of the wavelength.

9. **Huygens-Fresnel Principle**: This principle states that every point on a wavefront can be considered as a source of secondary spherical wavelets, which spread 
out in all directions at the same speed. The new wavefront is formed by the envelope of these secondary waves.

10. **Quantum Mechanics Perspective**: In quantum mechanics, light is understood as being composed of photons, discrete packets of energy that travel through space. 
Despite this particle-like behavior, when discussing the wave structure, we still consider it in terms of wave properties such as wavelength and frequency.

Understanding these aspects of light's wave structure is crucial for technologies like fiber optics, lasers, and even medical imaging techniques like MRI (Magnetic 
Resonance Imaging).
